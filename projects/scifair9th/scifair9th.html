A Thermoelectric Generator in Your Lap

February 2019

Proposal Paper
Results

I created a device which can absorb heat through the use of use of a TEG (Thermoelectric Generator 9mm by 9mm by 9mm). When there is a difference between the temperatures of the sides of the TEG, there will be a electric current created.
Here are some of the results I got from my experiment:

Low Heat Emission Rate:
The standard deviation of millivolts created for the low heat emissions for test 1 over the 10 minutes is 0.12 excluding the starting voltage. The standard deviation millivolts created for the low heat emissions for test 2 over the 10 minutes is 0.34 excluding the starting voltage. Due to this reason, test 1 is more reliable than test 2, however they are both still very reliable due to their distance to 0.
The percent change of millivolts created for the low heat emissions for test 1 between the highest and the lowest millivolts created is 44.44% increase. The percent change in millivolts created for the low heat emissions for test 2 is 333.33% increase excluding the starting voltage. Due to this evidence, test 2 has much more change than test 1.
My null hypothesis is: If a low amount of heat is exhausted, the electricity created would be around 1 mV. My alternative hypothesis is: If a low amount of heat is exhausted, then no electricity would be created. If my significance level is 0.05%, then the p-value for test 1 is 0.099%. From this, we can conclude that the alternative hypothesis can be rejected and the null hypothesis can be affirmed.
Once again, if my significance level is 0.05%, then the p-value for test 2 is 0.083%. For this reason, we can conclude that the alternative hypothesis can be rejected and the null hypothesis can be affirmed.
Medium Heat Emission Rate:
The standard deviation of millivolts created for the medium heat emissions for test 1 over the 10 minutes is 0.24 excluding the starting voltage. The standard deviation millivolts created for the medium heat emissions for test 2 over the 10 minutes is 0.31 excluding the starting voltage. Therefore, test 1 is more reliable than test 2, however they are both still somewhat reliable due to their distance to 0.
The percent change of millivolts created for the medium heat emissions for test 1 between the highest and the lowest millivolts created is 60% increase. The percent change in millivolts created for the medium heat emissions for test 2 is 142.86% increase excluding the starting voltage. Due to this evidence, test 2 has more change than test 1.
If my significance level is 0.05%, then the p-value for test 1 is 0.079%. Accordingly, we can conclude that the alternative hypothesis can be rejected and the null hypothesis can be affirmed.
Likewise, if my significance level is 0.05%, then the p-value for test 2 is 0.084%. Consequently, we can conclude that the alternative hypothesis can be rejected and the null hypothesis can be affirmed.

The standard deviation millivolts created for the medium heat emissions for test 2 over the 10 minutes is 0.31 excluding the starting voltage. The percent change in millivolts created for the high heat emissions for test 2 is 157% increase excluding the starting voltage. Therefore, test 1 has more change than test 2.
Additionally, if my significance level is 0.05%, then the p-value for test 1 is 0.09%. As a result, we can conclude that the alternative hypothesis can be rejected and the null hypothesis can be affirmed.
